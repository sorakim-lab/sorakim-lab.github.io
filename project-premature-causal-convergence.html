Premature Causal Convergence in RCA:
A Pilot Study of Early-Stage Reasoning under Closure Pressure

Reasoning continues to develop — but documentation demands a commitment before it has time to.

premature causal convergence closure pressure RCA reasoning pilot study time-based observation

A pilot study examining how GMP practitioners rapidly converge on a single causal explanation before formal RCA begins — and how closure pressure may narrow the reasoning space before systemic factors have been examined.

// 01

Background

In GMP environments, deviation investigations must be formally documented within defined timeframes. Quality teams operate under consistent pressure to close records — not because investigations are necessarily complete, but because open deviations create regulatory exposure and workflow bottlenecks.

This creates closure pressure: a structural condition in which the timeline for committing to an explanation is set by documentation requirements rather than by the state of the investigator's reasoning. The result is that investigators may be documenting a root cause before they have had sufficient time to consider alternatives.

// structural conditions
  • Documentation deadlines precede reasoning completion
  • Open deviations create regulatory and workflow pressure
  • Investigation records reflect first plausible explanation, not most complete one
  • Systemic contributors less likely to surface under time pressure
// HCI reframing

Premature causal convergence is not a training failure or an individual competence issue. It is a structural consequence of how investigation workflows are designed. This reframes the problem as a design question: how can investigation systems protect reasoning space within existing documentation timelines?

In actual GMP practice, most investigators spend far less than 10 minutes on initial causal analysis — not because they are careless, but because the system demands closure before reasoning has time to develop.
// 02

Method

A structured paper-based reasoning task with no digital tool or scaffold — a deliberate methodological choice. Using a scaffold would confound the observation, making it impossible to separate natural reasoning development from tool-assisted reasoning. The goal was to observe what unassisted causal reasoning looks like under a naturalistic time constraint.

01
scenario

An ELISA assay result exceeded its specification limit — a common GMP deviation type. Participants were asked to identify possible causes freely, without being directed toward any particular explanation category.

02
recording template — 4 steps

Step 1: initial cause written immediately. Step 2: additional causes added during 5–10 minute thinking period. Step 3: connections between causes described. Step 4: explicit comparison of initial vs. revised explanation — what changed and why.

03
time-point observation

Three observation points: 0 min (immediate), 5 min (mid-window), 10 min (end). Each point captured cause count, cause type, and whether explanation had changed.

04
why no prototype

The paper format preserved the naturalistic reasoning condition. That baseline observation — what reasoning looks like without structural support — is what motivates the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder as a design response.

// 03

Empirical Findings

Across five participants, initial causal reasoning was uniformly singular: all proposed only one likely cause at the outset, although the content of that cause varied across method-, equipment-, and human-related explanations. The key issue is not simply blame attribution, but early causal convergence itself.

0 min — initial
1 cause
All 5 participants. Cause types varied — assay method, equipment, environmental factor, reagent, operator action — but all were structurally singular.
5 min — expanding
2–3 causes
Most participants added at least one additional cause. Some began connecting causes, noting dependencies and contributing conditions.
10 min — revised
5/5 revised
All participants changed or expanded their initial explanation. Several reprioritised — moving procedural or systemic causes above their original explanation.
// cause type diversity

Initial causes varied across participants: assay method error, equipment malfunction, environmental contamination, reagent quality issue, and operator action. Despite this content diversity, the structural pattern was identical — single-hypothesis initial framing, followed by expansion over time.

// revision direction

When participants revised, they consistently moved toward more systemic framings. One participant who began with "operator error" later added "the SOP didn't make this step clear enough." This shift — from individual to system — only emerged with time, not at the outset.

In actual GMP investigation practice, most investigators spend far less than 10 minutes on initial causal analysis before documentation begins. This study suggests that the explanations being formally documented may reflect reasoning that has not yet had time to fully develop.

// implication

If reasoning continues to develop meaningfully within a 10-minute window, and documentation typically begins before that window closes, then investigation records may systematically underrepresent causal complexity.

// design response

This is not an argument against timeliness. It is an argument for designing investigation workflows that protect reasoning space within those timelines — which is precisely what the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder attempts.

Connection to RCA Scaffolder

This pilot study establishes the problem that the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder is designed to address. If premature causal convergence is a structural condition — produced by closure pressure, not individual failure — then the design response should be structural too.

// companion project · design response

This study shows what reasoning looks like before a scaffold exists — uniformly singular at outset, expanding only when given time that real GMP environments typically don't protect.

The RCA Reasoning Scaffolder shows what happens when structural support is provided: 80% of participants considered new causes they hadn't thought of before, and 15/15 reported the scaffold actively preserved reasoning breadth.

// 06

Paper in Progress

This pilot study is being developed into a paper on premature causal convergence, closure pressure, and the design of investigation workflows in regulated environments. The paper contributes a behavioral observation to the broader research program: early-stage causal reasoning in GMP investigation is structurally convergent, and the conditions that produce that convergence are design-addressable.

The paper also contributes methodologically — using time-point observation of unassisted reasoning to study investigation behavior without requiring deployment inside validated systems.

// study design
  • Paper-based structured reasoning task
  • Three-point time observation (0 / 5 / 10 min)
  • Qualitative coding of cause type and revision direction
  • Post-task structured questions
// research framing
  • Premature causal convergence in regulated investigation
  • Closure pressure as structural condition
  • Time-point reasoning observation methodology
  • Empirical foundation for scaffold design
