Premature Causal Convergence

A Pilot Study of Early-Stage Reasoning under Closure Pressure

This project examines how early-stage reasoning changes over time during RCA-style investigation tasks, and how closure pressure can narrow causal reasoning before systemic factors have been examined.

// participants
5
Pilot reasoning study with timed reflection intervals
// study format
3
Timepoints: initial, +5 min, +10 min
// key signal
10
Minutes changed the breadth of causal reasoning
// 01

Background

Root cause analysis is often treated as a neutral reasoning task. In practice, however, deviation investigations take place under expectations of speed, closure, and documentation readiness. These conditions can shape how reasoning unfolds before investigators have had time to consider broader contributing factors.

The result is that investigators may document a root cause before they have fully examined procedural, contextual, or organizational contributors. This pilot study was designed to observe that early-stage reasoning process directly, before any interface intervention was introduced.

Reasoning continues to develop — but documentation demands a commitment before it has time to.
// 02

Method

Participants were given the same investigation-style case and asked to explain the cause at three timepoints: immediately, after five minutes, and after ten minutes. The purpose was to observe whether additional time changed the breadth, direction, or confidence of their reasoning.

timepoint_01
0 minutes
Immediate explanation based on first interpretation of the case.
timepoint_02
+5 minutes
Participants continued reflecting without additional prompts, allowing alternative contributors to emerge.
timepoint_03
+10 minutes
Final reasoning state recorded to assess whether causal breadth changed further over time.
// rationale

This project was intentionally conducted without a prototype interface. The goal was to establish an empirical baseline: whether reasoning itself expands over short intervals before any design intervention attempts to support it.

// implication

If reasoning naturally broadens with time, then premature closure in investigation settings may reflect environmental pressure rather than fixed cognitive limitation.

// 03

Findings

At the initial timepoint, participants tended to converge on a single salient cause. With additional time, several began introducing procedural, contextual, or organizational explanations that were absent from their first response. The most notable changes occurred between the initial response and the five-minute mark.

By ten minutes, most participants had either broadened their explanation or become more explicit about uncertainty. This suggests that early closure may be less a property of the investigator than a consequence of constrained reasoning time.

// 04

Results & Implications

This pilot does not claim statistical generalizability. Its contribution is observational: it shows that investigation reasoning can change meaningfully over short intervals, even without new information. That pattern matters because regulated documentation systems often demand closure during precisely that unstable period.

// empirical implication
  • Reasoning breadth is time-sensitive
  • Early explanations may underrepresent systemic contributors
  • Closure pressure may distort causal interpretation
// design implication
  • Investigation environments may need structured support before closure
  • Reasoning scaffolds can be justified empirically, not only conceptually
  • Time-sensitive convergence should be treated as a UX issue
// 05

Connection to the Companion Project

This pilot project functions as the empirical observation layer for the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder. If premature convergence appears before broader contributors are considered, then the design challenge becomes how to keep investigation reasoning open longer without violating closure expectations.

// companion project
RCA Reasoning Scaffolder
The companion project translates this observation into a structured interface intervention. In the scaffolder study, 80% of participants considered new causes after using the scaffold, suggesting that the convergence observed here can be actively resisted through design.
