Premature Accountability Convergence: Inscription, Closure Pressure, and the Organization of Responsibility in Regulated Investigations

Accountability in regulated investigations does not emerge neutrally from the available evidence. It tends to converge — through documentary form, closure pressure, and procedural expectation — onto identifiable actors before alternative causal accounts have been meaningfully compared. Drawing on inscription studies and the sociology of audit and accountability (cf. Power 1997; Bowker & Star 1999), this paper argues that such convergence is a structural property of investigative environments, and that AI-mediated representations intensify rather than introduce it. This condition is conceptualized as Premature Accountability Convergence (PAC): not a cognitive bias, but a property of how inscription practices, closure pressure, and documentary form together constrain interpretive space.

Two empirical components ground this argument. A document analysis of FDA Warning Letters (N = 50, 2016–2025) shows that while attribution language varies — for example, “failure to” versus “inadequate” — accountability converges onto the regulated firm in nearly all observed violation cases (159/160). This suggests that documentary form itself compresses interpretive diversity into administratively actionable attribution, largely independent of the causal framing embedded within individual records. A timed pilot study (N = 20) further indicates that, under closure pressure, investigation reasoning tends to narrow toward single-cause explanations before broader contributing conditions are examined, pointing to convergence as a property of the investigative environment rather than of individual reasoning failure. Additional analysis demonstrates that when explicit comparison among alternative explanations is sustained prior to closure, causal accounts broaden and the scope of accountability correspondingly expands, suggesting that the conditions under which responsibility stabilizes are contingent rather than fixed.

These findings reframe what is at stake in the organization of regulated investigation. TechnoPower operates here not through explicit authority, but through the temporal architecture of accountability: the inscription practices and documentary arrangements that shape when alternatives remain visible and when they recede. From this perspective, counter-convergence can be understood as one site at which these arrangements may be made visible and, potentially, reconfigured.
