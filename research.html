Research Direction

I study how documentation systems, procedural environments, and support tools shape human judgment in high-stakes regulated work. My research focuses on usability, cognition, traceability, and decision support in settings where compliance requirements and human reasoning must coexist.

HCI regulatory UX procedural cognition decision support

Main domain: GMP documentation, investigations, review workflows, and inspection-facing records.

Research interest: how procedural systems can become more usable, interpretable, and cognitively sustainable without compromising regulatory rigor.

Current bridge: linking design questions in human-centered systems with conceptual work on responsibility, closure, and AI-mediated support.

Research areas

what I study across projects and papers
area 01

Regulatory UX

I examine how people navigate dense documentation, constrained workflows, and inspection-facing records in regulated environments where usability is often treated as secondary.

area 02

Procedural cognition

I study how people interpret instructions, manage ambiguity, and reason through procedural tasks when documents and systems place cognitive demands on everyday work.

area 03

Workflow visibility

I explore ways to make review interaction, traceability, bottlenecks, and document relationships more visible without altering validated systems directly.

area 04

Decision support in high-stakes systems

I am interested in how support tools, reasoning scaffolds, and AI-mediated representations shape judgment, interpretation, and responsibility under procedural pressure.

Core research questions

agenda-centered, not paper-centered
question 01

How do documents shape judgment?

I ask how procedural language, document architecture, and review structures distribute interpretive burden across workers in regulated settings.

question 02

How do workflows shape interpretation?

I study how review loops, traceability systems, and investigation formats influence what becomes visible, actionable, or cognitively difficult in everyday work.

question 03

How can support systems help without oversimplifying?

I explore human-centered tools that support navigation and reasoning while preserving interpretive openness instead of forcing premature closure.

question 04

What changes under AI mediation?

I investigate how AI systems may accelerate decision closure, compress interpretation, and reshape accountability in high-stakes procedural environments.

Methods

mixed methods for procedural environments
qualitative

Field-based observation and close reading

I use field observation, document analysis, and qualitative coding to understand how people actually work through regulated documentation and investigative processes.

computational

Low-code and computational analysis

I use Excel and Python to structure metadata, visualize workflow patterns, map cognitive pathways, and analyze document-level patterns in procedural systems.

Current work

empirical extensions in progress

FDA document analysis

My current empirical project examines how responsibility becomes stabilized within regulatory investigation documents. Using FDA Warning Letters and selected Form 483 observations from drug manufacturing inspections, I analyze how agencies articulate responsibility, enforcement reasoning, and corrective action expectations in response to observed violations.

This project gives me a public document corpus for studying how procedural systems translate ambiguity into actionable decisions and how responsibility becomes legible in real-world regulatory settings.

current role in the program: connecting document analysis, investigation reasoning, and accountability under high-stakes procedural conditions

Method and analytic focus

  • Dataset: FDA Warning Letters with matched Form 483 observations when available
  • Unit of analysis: violation or observation blocks within inspection documents
  • Approach: qualitative document analysis combining close reading and structured coding
  • Focus: attribution language, procedural framing, corrective action expectations, and convergence patterns in regulatory reasoning
broader aim: grounding conceptual questions in observable document systems while linking papers and design directions to a shared empirical domain
