Research Program

My research examines how responsibility becomes structured and stabilized in regulated sociotechnical systems through documentation, procedural infrastructures, investigative routines, and computational mediation.

Conceptual arc

Current work

Regulatory document analysis

Current empirical work analyzes FDA Warning Letters and selected Form 483 observations from drug manufacturing inspections. These regulatory documents provide a rare public record of how responsibility is articulated and stabilized within formal investigation narratives.

The study examines how responsibility convergence emerges in regulatory enforcement texts and how investigative reasoning translates complex sociotechnical failures into administratively actionable accountability.

Method

  • Dataset: FDA Warning Letters (drug manufacturing)
  • Supplementary documents: matched Form 483 observations
  • Unit of analysis: violation / observation blocks
  • Approach: qualitative document analysis and structured coding
  • Focus: responsibility attribution, procedural framing, corrective action language

Anticipated Accountability Convergence (AAC)

AAC conceptualizes accountability as a prospective structuring condition in organizational systems. The framework explains how procedural infrastructures and documentation regimes align interpretive expectations before action occurs.

Full paper manuscript in preparation for submission to the National Communication Association (NCA).

Cognitive Friction in Procedural Texts (CF)

CF examines how procedural language structures judgment demands during execution. The project studies how documentation architecture distributes interpretive burden across actors performing regulated work.

Expanded empirical corpus and annotation reliability testing currently in progress.

Responsibility Convergence (RC)

RC explains why investigations recurrently stabilize responsibility at identifiable actors even when failures are distributed across sociotechnical systems.

Premature Accountability Convergence (PAC)

PAC analyzes how AI-mediated systems reshape the timing of accountability stabilization by compressing interpretive space in organizational investigations.

Abstract in preparation for submission to the Society for Social Studies of Science (4S).
