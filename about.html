About

I study how responsibility becomes structured, stabilized, and accelerated within organizational systems. My work focuses on how documentation, procedural infrastructures, investigative routines, and computational representations shape accountability in regulated sociotechnical environments.

Core themes: responsibility, accountability, documentation, investigation, sociotechnical systems, and AI-mediated organizational judgment.
Empirical grounding: regulated environments shaped by procedural texts, quality systems, and accountability infrastructures.

Research Focus

My research examines the mechanisms through which responsibility becomes aligned before action, embedded within procedural structures during execution, stabilized through investigation after events, and increasingly reshaped through computational mediation.

Across highly regulated environments such as pharmaceuticals and other compliance-intensive sectors, accountability rarely emerges spontaneously. It is produced through documentation regimes, procedural texts, investigative infrastructures, and evaluative systems that define what counts as responsible action.

Research Program

My current research program develops a connected framework for understanding how responsibility evolves across the organizational lifecycle.

  • Anticipated Accountability Convergence (AAC) — how accountability expectations align before action.
  • Cognitive Friction in Procedural Texts (CF) — how procedural language distributes interpretive burden during execution.
  • Responsibility Convergence (RC) — how investigations stabilize responsibility after events.
  • Premature Accountability Convergence (PAC) — how AI-mediated representations accelerate responsibility stabilization.
AAC → CF → RC → PAC

Background

My professional background is in pharmaceutical quality systems, where I worked extensively with regulated documentation, method verification processes, technology transfer, and organizational investigation structures. That experience shaped my interest in how documentation architectures and accountability systems organize reasoning under constraint.

Building on this background, my research explores the sociotechnical dynamics of responsibility across documents, procedures, investigations, and AI-mediated infrastructures.

personal note

A little more personally

I am especially interested in the moments when organizational order looks stable on paper but remains interpretively fragile in practice.

Many of my questions come from documentation-heavy environments where rules, records, and accountability structures are everywhere, yet judgment still has to happen under uncertainty.

I like research that treats documents, procedures, and infrastructures not as background paperwork, but as active sites where responsibility gets shaped.
